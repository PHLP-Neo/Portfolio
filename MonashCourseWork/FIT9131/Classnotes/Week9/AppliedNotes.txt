Abstration:

Important considerations when designing a class are deciding what attributes and behaviours the 
class should have and what should be made available to a user of the class. To do this we use a 
process of abstraction. Abstraction is concerned with the reduction of detail to achieve a 
simplified representation of the whole. The process of abstraction in Java involves selecting the
essential characteristics for a class, ignoring any irrelevant details, and then displaying only 
the required details to the user.  

Using the process of abstraction we create a model that is a simplification of the real situation,
which is strictly relevant to the problem to be solved. Abstraction in Java brings a focus to what
information the object must contain and what must be displayed to the user. Abstraction provides a
way to deal with complexity and is one of the main theoretical principles of object-oriented 
programming.

8.2.3 Modularisation:

Complementing the concept of abstraction, which we discussed in the previous slide, is the concep
of modularisation. Modularisation is the process of dividing a problem to be solved into smaller 
and well-defined parts that can be built and tested separately and can interact in well defined 
ways. 

Modularisation is a way of dealing with complexity. In a large software application, it is 
impossible for one person to know all the details of the application or have the expertise 
necessary to solve all the aspects of the application. Using modularisation, a problem can be 
broken down into components that can be solved by one or a small group of programmers with 
expertise in that particular area.

Definition: Modularisation is the process of dividing a whole into well-defined parts that can be 
built and examined separately, and that interact in well-defined ways.

To illustrate the concept of modularisation we will look at an example of how it might be applied 
in the design of a car.  A car is a complicated piece of machinery. Designing a car involves 
people with different expertise, for example, mechanical engineers, electrical engineers, paint 
technicians, and interior designers. However, a car is made up of a number of components that can 
be considered separately. For example:

	- engine
	- gearbox
	- radiator
	- wheels
	- interior

Collectively, all the components represent a single entity of a car. However, using modularisation
, we can break down the complex car into smaller components that can be designed by a small team 
with expertise in that component.

The reason for using this type of approach when writing a computer program is that it allows for 
large and often complex programs to be split into smaller independent components that can work 
independently to satisfy a specific purpose, but also collectively fulfil a higher purpose.

In programming, modularisation and abstraction are often used to deal with complexity. As programs
become larger and more complicated these techniques become increasingly important. As new 
programmers, we will be adopting the concept of modularisation a lot more than abstraction as our 
programs are not going to be as complex. However, it is important to understand both concepts and 
how they can work together to ensure our program is well designed.
